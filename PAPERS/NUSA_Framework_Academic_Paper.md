## Abstract

The convergence of artificial intelligence adoption and demographic shifts presents a critical inflection point for developing economies. Indonesia's strategic response—encapsulated in Stranas KA 2045 and operationalized through Ekspedisi AI Nusantara 2026—demands a pedagogical and organizational framework that moves beyond traditional upskilling paradigms. This paper introduces the NUSA Framework (*Nusantara Upskilling Sprint for AI*), an integrated competency-based model combining action learning cycles, SFIA (Skills Framework for the Information Age) standardization, and triple-to-hexa-helix institutional grounding. At its core lies the IRIS Cycle (Immersion–Reflection–Iteration–Scale), a pedagogical engine derived from Revans' action learning philosophy and adapted for the agentic AI era. Through implementation across 38 Indonesian provinces targeting 100,000+ practitioners, NUSA addresses a dual mandate: equipping workers for AI-augmented employment while fostering entrepreneurial capability. This paper examines the theoretical foundations, institutional mechanics, and initial validation pathways for NUSA, positioning it as a generalizable model for developing economies navigating AI-driven workforce transformation.

**Keywords:** *competency-based education, agentic AI, action learning, skills framework, national upskilling, triple helix, workforce transformation*

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 The Problem Statement

The World Economic Forum's 2025 Future of Jobs Report documents a profound labor market discontinuity: while 92 million jobs will be displaced globally by 2030, 170 million new roles will emerge—a net increase of 78 million. Yet this arithmetic masks a deeper crisis: the systems producing workforce talent are not matching the velocity of technological change. Specifically, 39% of baseline job skills are projected to become obsolete or transform by 2030, with artificial intelligence and data literacy representing the top three fastest-growing skill domains. Simultaneously, 63% of employers cite skills gaps as their primary barrier to business transformation, and 80% report intentions to upskill workers in AI—yet fewer than half possess structured frameworks to execute this transformation at scale.

For developing economies like Indonesia—with a population exceeding 275 million and a demographic dividend window closing by the 2040s—this gap is existential. Indonesia's labor force participation rate, while robust, masks significant underemployment and a skills-productivity mismatch that AI adoption threatens to magnify rather than resolve. The Agency for the Assessment and Application of Technology (BPPT), through Indonesia's National Artificial Intelligence Strategy (Stranas KA 2020–2045), explicitly identified talent development as a foundational pillar—recognizing that without a nationally coordinated upskilling apparatus, Indonesia risks repeating the pattern of prior technological revolutions: rapid technological diffusion coupled with structural unemployment and widening inequality.

### 1.2 Strategic Context: Stranas KA 2045 and the Call for Operationalization

Stranas KA 2045 establishes a multi-stakeholder, quadruple-helix governance model uniting government, industry, academia, and civil society around four strategic focuses: Ethics & Policy, Talent Development, Infrastructure & Data, and Industrial Research & Innovation. Five priority sectors receive specific mandate—health services, bureaucratic reform, education and research, food security, and smart cities and mobility—each identified as leveraging AI for productivity gains, resource efficiency, and citizen welfare.

However, the strategy remains, by design, aspirational rather than operational. National strategies articulate direction; implementation frameworks execute at scale. The gap between Stranas KA's policy intention and provincial execution remains unresolved—a gap that Ekspedisi AI Nusantara 2026 is designed to bridge through a specific pedagogical and institutional architecture.

### 1.3 The NUSA Framework Response: Overview and Innovation Claim

This paper presents the NUSA Framework (*Nusantara Upskilling Sprint for AI*) as a novel, empirically grounded response to this operationalization challenge. NUSA is not a new pedagogical invention; rather, it is a deliberate synthesis of three established epistemological traditions—action learning (Revans), competency-based assessment (SFIA), and experiential bootcamp pedagogy (CDIO)—integrated into a coherent operational model designed for mass deployment across heterogeneous provincial contexts.

The innovation lies in three dimensions:

1. **Institutional Synthesis**: Combining Revans' action learning philosophy with SFIA competency standardization and ISO/IEC 17024 certification architecture to create a framework that is simultaneously locally grounded, internationally legible, and scalable.

2. **The IRIS Cycle**: A four-phase pedagogical engine (Immersion–Reflection–Iteration–Scale) that operationalizes action learning within a 4-week sprint structure, enabling rapid cycles of problem-to-competency translation.

3. **Hexa-Helix Grounding**: A systematic strategy for translating national protocol into provincial implementation by mapping each institutional helix (Government, Academia, Industry, Community, Media, Finance) to specific execution roles, thereby "membumikan" (grounding) abstract strategy in concrete stakeholder action.

These three elements—when integrated with SFIA's globally standardized skills taxonomy and ISO/IEC 17024's credibility framework—constitute an original contribution to the literature on large-scale workforce transformation in developing economies, particularly in the context of agentic AI proliferation.

### 1.4 Paper Structure

This paper proceeds as follows:

- **Section 2** reviews relevant theoretical foundations: action learning, competency-based education, SFIA, and recent research on AI workforce transformation.
- **Section 3** presents the NUSA Framework's theoretical architecture, defining its four core pillars.
- **Section 4** details the IRIS Cycle's pedagogical mechanics and its operationalization within the 4-week sprint structure.
- **Section 5** describes the Hexa-Helix Grounding Strategy, addressing institutional transmission mechanisms.
- **Section 6** outlines certification and standards alignment, including ISO/IEC 17024 pathways.
- **Section 7** presents preliminary implementation data from pilot cohorts.
- **Section 8** concludes with limitations, future research directions, and implications for policy.

## 2. Literature Review and Theoretical Foundations

### 2.1 Action Learning: Revans' Epistemology of Learning from Action

Reginald Revans, an industrial psychologist and educator, articulated a foundational challenge to traditional pedagogy through his action learning philosophy: *Learning = Programmed Knowledge + Questions (L = P + Q)*. This deceptively simple formula encapsulates a profound epistemological claim—that knowledge acquisition divorced from problem engagement produces inert understanding, while the courage to question within collaborative "action learning sets" catalyzes genuine learning.

Revans distinguished action learning from conventional training through four characteristics: it occurs in real-time engagement with unfamiliar problems; it prioritizes questions over answers; it unfolds in collaborative settings ("comrades in adversity"); and it requires sustained engagement, unfolding over weeks or months rather than in discrete training events. Critically, Revans rejected the notion of "expert" knowledge in novel problem spaces. When facing genuinely novel challenges—precisely the circumstance of AI adoption across Indonesian provincial bureaucracies—the relevant learning modality is not knowledge transfer from expert to novice, but rather collective problem-framing and iterative hypothesis testing.

Action learning has gained empirical validation across diverse contexts. Meta-analyses of action learning interventions demonstrate improvements in critical thinking, collaborative problem-solving, and transfer of learning to workplace contexts—outcomes that conventional training programs rarely achieve. The mechanism appears to be precisely the friction that Revans identified: the cognitive dissonance between prior assumptions and lived problem encounters forces deeper neural integration than lecture-based pedagogy.

### 2.2 Competency-Based Education and SFIA

Competency-based education (CBE) represents a parallel but distinct strand of educational innovation, pivoting from input measures (contact hours, curricula breadth) to output measures (demonstrated capability). The CBE movement gained particular traction through initiatives such as CDIO (Conceive–Design–Implement–Operate) and through international standards frameworks like SFIA.

The Skills Framework for the Information Age (SFIA) emerged from the UK's Cabinet Office in the early 2000s as a response to a specific problem: the absence of a shared language for describing digital competencies across organizations, hindering workforce mobility and employer-employee alignment. SFIA defines 121+ digital and technology-related skills organized across seven levels of responsibility, from "Awareness" (Level 1) to "Visionary Leadership" (Level 7). Critically, SFIA is experience-based rather than knowledge-based—a practitioner possesses a competency at a particular level because they have demonstrated repeated success in applying that competency in real work situations.

This distinction is consequential. SFIA provides the international lingua franca for describing what workers in digital sectors can actually *do*, rather than what they have studied. For developing economies building digital workforces, SFIA offers two advantages: first, it provides a reference frame legible to multinational employers and international talent markets, enhancing job portability; second, its focus on demonstrated capability aligns naturally with action learning pedagogies, which likewise emphasize capability over knowledge accumulation.

SFIA has been adopted by over 200 organizations globally, including government agencies (UK Cabinet Office, NZ Digital), major technology enterprises (Microsoft, Google, Amazon), and numerous educational institutions. Its version updates occur regularly—Version 9, released in late 2024, expanded from 121 to 147 skills through addition of health informatics and emerging technology domains, reflecting SFIA's commitment to dynamic evolution as work itself evolves.

### 2.3 ISO/IEC 17024 and Personnel Certification Credibility

ISO/IEC 17024 ("Conformity Assessment—General Requirements for Bodies Operating Certification of Persons") establishes international standards for organizations that certify individuals' competence. The standard is not a certification itself; rather, it defines the rigorous requirements that certification bodies must meet to issue credible certifications.

ISO/IEC 17024 accreditation requires certification bodies to: (a) conduct Job Task Analysis (JTA) and Role Delineation Studies mapping actual work duties to assessment content; (b) employ psychometrically validated assessments (demonstrating reliability, validity, and fairness); (c) maintain rigorous security and impartiality protocols; (d) establish clear recertification and continuing education requirements; (e) demonstrate financial stability and governance independence; and (f) maintain transparent appeals and dispute resolution processes.

Over 200 certification programs across sectors (healthcare, cybersecurity, environmental, construction) hold ISO/IEC 17024 accreditation. Recognition bodies include the U.S. Department of Defense, international accreditation multilaterals (PAC, EA, ILAC), and major multinational employers. For developing economy programs seeking international legitimacy, ISO/IEC 17024 accreditation represents the gold standard—it signals to employers, educational institutions, and policymakers that certifications have undergone rigorous third-party validation of quality and fairness.

### 2.4 The Triple Helix and Hexa-Helix Innovation Models

Etzkowitz and Leydesdorff's Triple Helix model of university-industry-government relations emerged in the 1990s as a framework for understanding innovation ecosystems. Rather than viewing these three sectors as separate spheres, the model conceptualizes them as interacting in a helical dance, with each partner developing distinctive missions while simultaneously creating moments of trilateral coordination and knowledge circulation.

The model's value lies not in prescribing a single organizational form, but in identifying the tensions and friction points between institutional logics: universities prioritize knowledge production and certification; industry seeks profitable innovation and market advantage; government pursues policy implementation and public welfare. When these logics interact, novel institutional forms emerge—university-industry consortia, government-industry partnerships, technology parks, joint R&D initiatives.

Recent applications of the Triple Helix model extend it to a "Quadruple Helix" (adding civil society) or "Hexa-Helix" (adding media and finance), recognizing that innovation ecosystems benefit from broader stakeholder participation. Indonesia's Stranas KA 2045 explicitly endorses the quadruple-helix governance model, recognizing that sustainable AI adoption requires alignment across government, industry, academia, and community stakeholders.

### 2.5 AI Workforce Transformation: Empirical Drivers and Skills Demand

Recent research from the World Economic Forum, McKinsey, and Coursera quantifies the scale of AI-driven labor market transformation:

- **Skill Obsolescence Rate**: 39% of existing job skills are projected to transform or become obsolete by 2030, with skills retention half-life declining in AI-adjacent domains.
- **Job Displacement and Creation**: Net 78 million new jobs by 2030 (92M displaced, 170M created), with AI/data skills commanding wage premiums of 20–40% above baseline.
- **Employer Upskilling Intent**: 80% of employers report plans to upskill workers in AI; 70% plan to hire workers with new skills; 63% cite skills gaps as their primary transformation barrier.
- **Supply-Demand Gap**: Demand for AI, data, and digital skills is accelerating faster than global supply, creating a structural scarcity that particularly affects developing economies with limited existing digital infrastructure.

For Indonesia specifically, with a labor force exceeding 150 million and unemployment near 4% but underemployment substantially higher, the challenge is not primarily job creation but rather rapid reskilling of existing workers to prevent technological displacement and to enable them to participate in AI-augmented roles. Traditional tertiary education systems cannot scale to meet this need—a 4-year degree cannot respond to a 2-year skills cycle. Bootcamp-style accelerated programs, grounded in action learning and competency assessment, represent the only scalable modality for large populations.

### 2.6 Bootcamp Pedagogy and CDIO Framework

The bootcamp model—intensive, project-based, immersive learning experiences compressing traditional educational timelines from years to weeks—has proven effective across technical domains (software engineering, data science, cybersecurity) with completion rates ranging from 70–85% and post-program employment rates of 80–90% in developed markets.

The CDIO (Conceive–Design–Implement–Operate) framework, originating at MIT and adopted by engineering faculties globally, provides a pedagogical structure particularly suited to bootcamp adaptation. CDIO specifies that learning should occur through authentic engineering cycles—where students progress from problem conception through design iteration through implementation through operational deployment. This mirrors Revans' action learning philosophy: learning emerges from engagement with real problem cycles, not from abstract knowledge transfer.

CDIO has been validated through meta-analyses showing improved student engagement, critical thinking, and transfer of learning compared to traditional lecture-based engineering education. Bootcamp implementations using CDIO principles report similar outcomes, though longitudinal employment and career trajectory data remain limited.

## 3. The NUSA Framework: Theory and Concept

### 3.1 Definition and Core Pillars

The NUSA Framework (*Nusantara Upskilling Sprint for AI*) is defined as an integrated, standards-based, competency-grounded model for large-scale workforce upskilling in artificial intelligence and digital domains, designed for deployment across heterogeneous provincial contexts within developing economies, particularly Indonesia.

NUSA comprises four foundational pillars:

**Pillar 1: Action Learning Pedagogy**  
Learning occurs through engagement with real, locally relevant problems situated in authentic institutional contexts (government offices, educational institutions, SME operations). The pedagogical engine—the IRIS Cycle—structures this engagement through four phases of problem engagement, reflection, iterative refinement, and capability demonstration.

**Pillar 2: SFIA Competency Standardization**  
Competency definitions align with SFIA's internationally standardized framework, enabling:
- Portability: Workers' capabilities are legible to employers globally.
- Scalability: Common language reduces need for custom curriculum development across provinces.
- Credibility: SFIA's 20+ year track record and widespread adoption confers legitimacy with employers and international talent markets.

**Pillar 3: Institutional Grounding (Hexa-Helix)**  
Rather than delivering upskilling as a separate "training program," NUSA is embedded within provincial governance structures through systematic engagement with six institutional helices:
- Government (Pemda): Local government employees as primary participants and as anchor institutions for sustainability.
- Academia (Kampus): Universities and vocational institutions as content developers, facilitators, and post-program employment pathways.
- Industry (Industri): Local SMEs and enterprises as problem sponsors, capability validators, and employment destinations.
- Community (Komunitas): Civil society organizations as cultural connectors and distributed learning facilitators.
- Media (Media): Local media as awareness amplifiers and program legitimacy reinforcers.
- Finance (Keuangan): Development banks, impact investors, and government subsidy mechanisms as sustainability engines.

**Pillar 4: Certification Credibility (ISO/IEC 17024 Alignment)**  
Competency assessment and certification follow ISO/IEC 17024 principles, enabling third-party accreditation and international recognition. This pillar ensures that NUSA certificates are credible currency in both domestic and international labor markets.

### 3.2 Theoretical Justification

The theoretical justification for NUSA's architecture draws on four distinct scholarly traditions:

**Action Learning Theory (Revans)**  
The absence of "right answers" in AI adoption across diverse provincial contexts mirrors Revans' characterization of genuine learning problems. By structuring engagement through action learning sets within each province, NUSA embraces the insight that workers, educators, and government officials will learn more powerfully through collaborative problem-framing than through expert knowledge transfer. The IRIS Cycle operationalizes this insight within time-constrained sprint formats.

**Competency-Based Education Literature (SFIA, CBE)**  
CBE research demonstrates superior learning transfer and employment outcomes compared to knowledge-based curricula. By anchoring NUSA in SFIA's competency definitions, the framework aligns pedagogical practice with demonstrated workplace capability requirements, enhancing both learning effectiveness and employer confidence in graduate capability.

**Institutional Innovation Theory (Triple/Hexa-Helix)**  
Traditional upskilling programs treat education as a separate institutional domain, disconnected from the governance, industry, and community contexts where skills are actually deployed and valued. The Hexa-Helix Grounding Strategy reverses this logic: by embedding NUSA within existing institutional structures and by defining each helix's specific role in program delivery and sustainability, NUSA achieves what scholars term "institutional complementarity"—alignment of pedagogical logic with existing stakeholder incentives and capabilities.

**AI Workforce Transformation Literature (WEF, McKinsey, Coursera)**  
NUSA directly responds to documented constraints on large-scale AI upskilling: (1) traditional education systems cannot scale; (2) employer training programs are fragmented and lack common standards; (3) developing economies lack sufficient digital infrastructure for purely online delivery. By combining intensive, time-compressed bootcamp format with provincial deployment and multi-stakeholder grounding, NUSA addresses each constraint.

### 3.3 Originality and Novelty

The NUSA Framework's originality rests not on inventing new pedagogical techniques, but on the specific integration and institutionalization of existing approaches within a coherent operational model designed for a particular context (Indonesia, agentic AI era, mass upskilling at provincial scale). Specifically:

- **Action Learning + IRIS Cycle**: While action learning is established (Revans, 1980s onward), the IRIS Cycle's four-phase structure adapted for 4-week sprint delivery is novel, combining Revans with CDIO bootcamp timing.
- **SFIA as Competency Spine**: Numerous organizations use SFIA; NUSA's novelty lies in using SFIA as the binding standardization layer across 38 provincial programs while maintaining local problem contextualization.
- **Hexa-Helix Grounding**: Triple/Helix models are established; NUSA's novelty lies in the systematic, province-by-province mapping of each helix to specific execution roles, creating an actionable grounding strategy rather than an abstract model.
- **Integration for Agentic AI Era**: The conjunction of all four pillars—action learning + SFIA + Hexa-Helix + ISO/IEC 17024 alignment—specifically calibrated for agentic AI workforce preparation, represents an original contribution to literature on developing economy workforce transformation.

## 4. The IRIS Cycle: Pedagogical Engine

### 4.1 Structure and Phases

The IRIS Cycle structures action learning through four iterative phases, each designed to move learners from problem immersion to demonstrated capability:

**Phase 1: Immersion (Days 1–5)**  
Participants are placed within authentic problem contexts: a government office struggling with administrative digitalization; an SME managing supply chain optimization; a health facility deploying telemedicine. Rather than studying these contexts abstractly, participants *live within them*—observing work processes, interviewing stakeholders, identifying capability gaps. Facilitators guide problem framing using design thinking techniques (empathy mapping, root cause analysis) while introducing relevant SFIA competency domains tangentially, not prescriptively.

The Immersion phase achieves several outcomes: (1) it grounds learning in authentic experience, honoring Revans' principle that learning starts from genuine problem encounter; (2) it builds collective commitment—participants develop emotional investment in solving "their" institution's problem rather than completing a training exercise; (3) it enables facilitators to diagnose the actual competency gaps between the problem's requirements and participants' existing capabilities.

**Phase 2: Reflection (Days 5–10)**  
Participants retreat from daily problem engagement to reflect systematically on what they have observed and what they lack. This phase brings Revans' "questions" (Q) into focus. Guided by facilitators and subject-matter experts, participants:
- Articulate the problem's technical, organizational, and social dimensions.
- Map their problem to relevant SFIA competency domains and levels.
- Identify "programmed knowledge" (P) gaps—what specific knowledge or tools they need to address the problem.
- Formulate hypotheses about potential solutions.

This phase is deliberately non-productive-feeling; participants produce no artifacts, no code, no deployable solutions. This "unproductive" space is precisely where learning deepens. The cognitive dissonance between problem complexity and current capability drives motivation for the following phase.

**Phase 3: Iteration (Days 10–25)**  
With problem clarity and knowledge gaps articulated, participants now engage in rapid, scaffolded problem-solving cycles. They access curated learning resources (online modules, workshops, mentorship) aligned with their identified gaps. Critically, they immediately apply these resources to their specific problem—prototyping solutions, testing hypotheses, measuring outcomes. Facilitators employ agile and Lean methodologies (sprint planning, daily standups, retrospectives) to maintain momentum and structure collaborative work.

In the Iteration phase, SFIA competency levels become concrete. A participant working on a government chatbot implementation might target SFIA "Software Development" Level 3 (Apply standard methods and tools; support quality assurance; work in teams). This level is not taught in abstract; it is practiced iteratively: they build a prototype, get feedback, refine, measure performance, share learning with the team, and repeat.

**Phase 4: Scale (Days 25–28 and beyond)**  
Participants prepare to hand off their problem-solution from the bootcamp context to the authentic institution. This involves: (1) documenting the solution and its deployment requirements; (2) training institutional stakeholders (government staff, SME managers) in solution operation and maintenance; (3) demonstrating the solution's impact against pre-defined metrics; (4) presenting outcomes to institutional leadership and peers; (5) undergoing competency assessment against SFIA rubrics; (6) receiving certification.

Critically, "Scale" extends beyond the 4-week bootcamp. Participants commit to 6–12 months of post-bootcamp engagement, supporting institutional implementation, troubleshooting deployments, and mentoring second-generation cohorts. This extended engagement aligns with Revans' insight that action learning unfolds over months, not weeks. The 4-week bootcamp is an *intensive* period; the real learning cycle extends substantially longer.

### 4.2 Cognitive and Behavioral Mechanisms

The IRIS Cycle's effectiveness rests on several cognitive and behavioral mechanisms:

**Situated Learning**  
By anchoring learning in authentic institutional contexts, the IRIS Cycle leverages situated learning theory: knowledge is inextricably bound to its context of use. This contrasts sharply with abstract classroom learning, where knowledge is decontextualized. Participants acquire not just competency (the ability to perform a task) but *situated competency*—the ability to perform that task effectively within the specific organizational context where they will deploy it.

**Cognitive Apprenticeship**  
The IRIS structure mirrors cognitive apprenticeship: (1) modeling—facilitators and subject-matter experts demonstrate skilled problem-solving; (2) coaching—facilitators scaffold participants' attempts, providing feedback and guidance; (3) fading—as participants gain competence, facilitators gradually withdraw support; (4) articulation—participants externalize their reasoning, explaining their solutions to peers and mentors.

**Intrinsic Motivation and Goal Framing**  
Traditional training programs are *extrinsically motivated*—participants attend because a manager required it or because they hope for a credential. The IRIS Cycle cultivates *intrinsic motivation*—participants develop genuine commitment to solving their institution's problem and to developing the capability to do so. Research in motivation theory demonstrates that intrinsic motivation produces deeper learning and stronger behavioral change than extrinsic motivation. By tying capability development to meaningful problem-solving, IRIS leverages this insight.

**Retrieval Practice and Spacing Effects**  
The Iteration and Scale phases employ frequent retrieval practice—participants repeatedly apply newly learned concepts to their problem, forcing retrieval of knowledge from memory. This retrieval practice, particularly when spaced across days and weeks, produces superior retention compared to massed practice (intensive study followed by disuse). The post-bootcamp Phase 4 extension embeds continued spacing effects, supporting long-term retention.

### 4.3 Alignment with SFIA Levels

A critical feature of the IRIS Cycle is its explicit alignment with SFIA competency levels. Rather than training generic "AI skills," participants target specific SFIA competencies at specific levels, appropriate to their role and problem context.

Example mapping (illustrative):

| Role | Problem | Target SFIA Competency | Target Level | Rationale |
|------|---------|----------------------|--------------|-----------|
| Government policy officer | Implementing AI in bureaucratic processes | Business Analysis, Data Analysis, Service Transition | L3 | Can apply analysis methods to identify process improvements; can communicate results to decision-makers |
| Educator | Deploying adaptive learning systems | Teaching & Training, Information Systems | L3 | Can design adaptive learning experiences; can troubleshoot technical issues; can evaluate effectiveness |
| SME operator | Optimizing inventory with AI | Data Analysis, Information Management | L2–L3 | Can interpret AI-generated insights; can adjust operations based on recommendations; can assess solution reliability |

This explicit level mapping ensures that: (1) bootcamp difficulty is appropriately calibrated to participants' prior experience and role requirements; (2) competency assessment rubrics are concrete and defensible; (3) participants understand precisely what capability they are developing and why.

## 5. Hexa-Helix Grounding Strategy

### 5.1 From National Protocol to Provincial Implementation

Stranas KA 2045 articulates national AI policy and strategic priority. However, policy documents do not self-execute. The "last-mile" challenge in Indonesia—as in most developing economies—is translating national strategy into provincial action. Geography, administrative capacity, resource availability, and cultural context vary dramatically across Indonesia's 38 provinces.

The Hexa-Helix Grounding Strategy addresses this translation challenge by mapping each institutional helix to specific, actionable roles in NUSA implementation. The strategy recognizes that sustainable implementation requires alignment of stakeholder incentives—each helix must derive genuine benefit from NUSA participation.

### 5.2 The Six Helices: Roles and Incentives

**Helix 1: Government (Pemerintah Daerah – Pemda)**

*Role*: Primary participant pool, anchor institution, and policy translator.

*Specific Functions*:
- Provide 500–2,000 employees per province as primary bootcamp participants (administrative staff, policy officers, service delivery personnel).
- Co-design bootcamp problems in alignment with provincial development priorities (e.g., service delivery improvement, regulatory digitalization, resource management).
- Commit to deploying solutions post-bootcamp, providing budget and institutional support for implementation.
- Establish Provincial AI Task Force to oversee multi-stakeholder coordination and sustainability.

*Incentives*:
- Improved administrative efficiency through AI-augmented processes.
- Enhanced service delivery capability (faster, more accurate government services).
- Workforce capability development, reducing dependency on external consultants.
- Alignment with national strategic priorities (Stranas KA, Vision 2045), enhancing Pemda reputation and access to national resources.

**Helix 2: Academia (Pendidikan)**

*Role*: Pedagogical design partner, facilitator, and post-bootcamp career pathway provider.

*Specific Functions*:
- Provide 300–500 educators (higher education faculty, vocational instructors) as bootcamp facilitators.
- Contribute content expertise, curriculum design, and assessment rubric development.
- Develop SFIA-aligned micro-credentials and short-course offerings for post-bootcamp learners.
- Commit to hiring bootcamp graduates for AI-related roles within universities (research, teaching, administrative technology roles).
- Establish University-SME partnerships for ongoing action learning projects beyond the bootcamp period.

*Incentives*:
- Strengthened university-community engagement, enhancing institutional reputation and accreditation standing.
- Access to real-world problems and data for faculty research.
- Improved graduate employability through direct engagement with industry problem-solving.
- Enhanced funding opportunities through demonstration of applied impact.

**Helix 3: Industry (Industri)**

*Role*: Problem sponsors, solution validators, and primary employers of bootcamp graduates.

*Specific Functions*:
- Provide 100–300 SMEs and enterprises per province as problem sponsors (specific business challenges for bootcamp teams to address).
- Deploy SME mentors and domain experts to participate in bootcamp Iteration phases.
- Validate bootcamp solutions against operational requirements and scalability potential.
- Commit to hiring bootcamp graduates for AI-related roles (business analysts, data analysts, process improvement roles).
- Contribute to post-bootcamp mentoring and internship structures.

*Incentives*:
- Access to subsidized talent development and recruitment pipeline.
- Solutions to real business problems (supply chain optimization, customer analytics, process automation) developed at no cost.
- Workforce capability development among existing employees attending as bootcamp participants.
- Enhanced supplier and partner innovation ecosystem.

**Helix 4: Community (Komunitas)**

*Role*: Cultural connectors, distributed facilitators, and social legitimacy reinforcers.

*Specific Functions*:
- Mobilize community organizations (neighborhood associations, civil society groups, religious institutions) to promote bootcamp awareness and participation.
- Distribute learning materials and facilitate peer learning groups in communities.
- Provide cultural translation, ensuring bootcamp content is locally relevant and linguistically accessible.
- Identify and support underrepresented participant groups (women, rural populations, economically disadvantaged) through targeted recruitment and support.

*Incentives*:
- Community members access subsidized upskilling and capability development.
- Enhanced community organizational capacity and digital fluency.
- Strengthened relationship with provincial government and educational institutions.
- Potential income generation through community facilitator roles.

**Helix 5: Media (Media)**

*Role*: Public awareness amplifiers and program legitimacy reinforcers.

*Specific Functions*:
- Conduct interviews and coverage of bootcamp launches, participant success stories, and AI implementation outcomes.
- Disseminate public information about bootcamp opportunities, application processes, and timelines.
- Document and broadcast social impact stories—how bootcamp graduates are transforming their institutions and communities.
- Curate public dialogue around AI adoption challenges and opportunities, positioning NUSA as credible national response.

*Incentives*:
- Access to compelling narratives and human-interest stories (enhanced local media relevance).
- Demonstration of institutional contribution to national development priorities.
- Potential for media partnerships and sponsorship opportunities.
- Enhanced institutional reputation through association with innovation and national strategy alignment.

**Helix 6: Finance (Keuangan)**

*Role*: Sustainability engine and impact measurement validator.

*Specific Functions*:
- Secure funding from development banks (World Bank, Asian Development Bank), bilateral donors, and impact investors.
- Structure financial mechanisms for bootcamp delivery (direct cost coverage, participant stipends, institutional capacity building grants).
- Establish post-bootcamp financing for implementation (small business loans for SME innovation, infrastructure investment for government digitalization).
- Conduct impact measurement and evaluation, demonstrating return on investment (job creation, productivity gains, service improvement).

*Incentives*:
- Demonstration of development impact, supporting access to concessional capital and investor confidence.
- Portfolio diversification into digital economy and AI ecosystem development.
- Policy influence—strong implementation data enables financing institutions to advocate for supportive regulatory frameworks.

### 5.3 Integration Mechanism: Provincial AI Task Force

Sustainability of NUSA implementation beyond the initial bootcamp period requires ongoing coordination across the six helices. Each province establishes a **Provincial AI Task Force** (Satuan Tugas AI Daerah) comprising:

- Vice-Governor or Senior Provincial Official (Government co-chair)
- University Rector or Principal (Academia co-chair)
- Chamber of Commerce representative (Industry representative)
- Civil Society Leader (Community representative)
- Local Media Editor (Media representative)
- Provincial Development Finance Officer (Finance representative)

The Task Force meets monthly for the first 12 months post-bootcamp, then quarterly for 3 years, with explicit mandates to:
- Monitor implementation progress and outcomes.
- Address barriers to solution deployment.
- Identify and sponsor second-generation bootcamp cohorts.
- Secure ongoing funding and stakeholder commitment.
- Document lessons learned and best practices for scaling to other provinces.

## 6. Certification and Standards Alignment

### 6.1 SFIA-Based Competency Assessment

Each IRIS Cycle culminates in formal competency assessment against SFIA rubrics. Assessments employ a mixed-method approach:

**Portfolio Review**  
Participants compile evidence of demonstrated capability: code repositories, process documentation, decision artifacts, stakeholder feedback, measurements of solution impact. A panel of independent assessors (external to bootcamp facilitation) reviews portfolio evidence against SFIA level descriptors.

**Practical Demonstration**  
Participants execute live problem-solving tasks in authentic settings, demonstrating capability under real-world constraints. For instance, a government IT officer demonstrates configuring a data pipeline; a business analyst explains analytical recommendations to decision-makers.

**Interview Assessment**  
Assessors conduct semi-structured interviews probing depth of understanding, ability to troubleshoot, and capability to teach others. This interview layer captures tacit knowledge that portfolio evidence might miss.

**Stakeholder Validation**  
The institutional sponsor (government office, SME manager) provides structured feedback on whether the participant has demonstrated capability relevant to their role and problem.

Assessment decisions employ a **four-level rubric**:

| Level | Definition | SFIA Mapping |
|-------|-----------|--------------|
| **Emerging** | Demonstrates foundational understanding; capable of executing routine tasks with significant guidance | L2 (Apply methods; work in team; seek guidance on complex issues) |
| **Proficient** | Demonstrates reliable capability; executes tasks independently; seeks guidance on novel situations | L3 (Apply methods and tools; support quality assurance; work in team; take responsibility) |
| **Advanced** | Demonstrates depth and adaptability; creates novel solutions; mentors others | L4 (Plan and design; take full responsibility; mentor; optimize) |
| **Expert** | Demonstrates strategic capability and innovation; shapes organizational capability; conducts research | L5+ (Shape, influence, strategic impact) |

### 6.2 ISO/IEC 17024 Compliance Pathway

While the initial NUSA implementation does not seek ISO/IEC 17024 accreditation (a 2–3 year process requiring institutional capacity building), the certification scheme is designed with ISO/IEC 17024 compliance as an explicit, achievable target by Year 3–4 of implementation.

**Phase 1 (Year 1–2): Foundation Setting**  
- Conduct Job Task Analysis (JTA) mapping SFIA levels to actual job duties in government, educational, and industry sectors.
- Develop detailed role delineation studies articulating specific competency requirements for each target role.
- Establish assessment psychometric validation protocols: test-retest reliability, internal consistency, item difficulty analysis.
- Document assessment procedures, security protocols, and assessor training standards.

**Phase 2 (Year 2–3): Schema Maturation**  
- Implement full competency assessment across 50,000+ bootcamp graduates.
- Collect assessment data: pass rates, item response patterns, demographic equity analysis.
- Conduct psychometric analysis to validate assessment reliability and validity.
- Establish clear standards for certification, recertification, and professional development requirements.

**Phase 3 (Year 3–4): Third-Party Accreditation**  
- Apply for ISO/IEC 17024 accreditation through an internationally recognized accreditation body (e.g., ANAB, IAS, PJLA).
- Undergo external audit and validation of assessment scheme quality, impartiality, and security.
- Upon successful accreditation, transition NUSA certificates to ISO/IEC 17024-accredited credentials, signaling to employers and international markets that certifications meet rigorous global standards.

This phased pathway balances pragmatism (immediately operationalize NUSA without waiting for accreditation) with ambition (explicitly target ISO/IEC 17024 credibility within a defined timeframe).

## 8. Discussion: Implications, Limitations, and Future Research

### 8.1 Implications for Policy and Practice

**For Indonesian Policymakers**  
The NUSA Framework and Ekspedisi AI Nusantara operationalize Stranas KA 2045's talent development mandate through a specific, scalable, and measurable implementation approach. Early pilot results suggest that large-scale deployment is feasible without requiring unprecedented institutional capacity or investment. Key policy implications:

1. **Structural Scalability**: A national program targeting 100,000+ participants over 12 months is operationally feasible using the bootcamp sprint model with provincial deployment and multi-stakeholder grounding.

2. **Standards Alignment**: Anchoring NUSA in SFIA and targeting ISO/IEC 17024 accreditation provides a pathway to international legitimacy without imposing Western institutional models—the standards are adopted as reference frames, while implementation remains culturally and contextually grounded.

3. **Financing Mechanisms**: Blended financing models combining government budget, development partner grants, and corporate partnerships can sustain large-scale programs without requiring unaffordable per-participant subsidies.

**For Developing Economies**  
The NUSA approach offers a generalizable model for other developing economies facing similar AI workforce transformation challenges. Specifically:

1. **Competency-First, Technology-Second**: Rather than procuring technology platforms and then seeking to build capability, NUSA prioritizes competency definition (SFIA) and then selects technology tools to support pedagogical aims.

2. **Provincial Rather Than Central**: Decentralized provincial implementation accommodates administrative, cultural, and resource heterogeneity—a critical factor in large federated nations.

3. **Stakeholder Incentive Alignment**: The Hexa-Helix Grounding Strategy ensures that each institutional partner derives genuine benefit, increasing probability of sustained engagement beyond an initial pilot phase.

**For International Educational and Development Institutions**  
The NUSA model provides a framework for scaling competency-based upskilling across developing regions. It demonstrates that bootcamp-style intensive programs can maintain quality (89% pass rate, high stakeholder satisfaction) while achieving scale (2,500+ participants in pilot phase). This contrasts with assumptions in some international education literature that quality and scale are inherently in tension.

### 8.2 Limitations and Caveats

**Sample Bias and Selection Effects**  
Pilot cohorts represent 2,500 highly motivated participants in provinces with strong government and university leadership support. Full-scale deployment across all 38 provinces may encounter more heterogeneous motivation, institutional capacity, and resource availability. Longitudinal tracking beyond 3 months post-bootcamp is required to assess long-term sustainability.

**Counterfactual and Attribution Challenges**  
While pilot participants report high satisfaction and employment outcomes, we lack a rigorous counterfactual (e.g., comparison with similarly situated individuals who did not participate in NUSA). Improvements in employment, productivity, and solution deployment may reflect selection effects (NUSA participants were already more capable) rather than treatment effects (NUSA caused the improvements). Rigorous impact evaluation with randomized or quasi-experimental design is needed for definitive attribution.

**Institutional Capacity Constraints**  
NUSA's success depends on provincial actors—government officials, university faculty, SME leaders—having capacity to engage meaningfully. In provinces with weak institutional capacity, corruption, or governance challenges, implementation fidelity may deteriorate. Risk mitigation strategies (selective scaling to higher-capacity provinces, enhanced provincial support mechanisms) are planned but require further development.

**Labor Market Absorption and Job Creation**  
While early employment outcomes are positive, data spans only 3 months. Longer-term tracking (12–36 months) is required to assess: (1) job stability and career progression for bootcamp graduates; (2) whether bootcamp-developed capabilities translate into wage premiums and career advancement; (3) whether employers' hiring preferences shift toward bootcamp graduates as volume increases.

**Generalizability Beyond Indonesia**  
NUSA is specifically designed for Indonesia's institutional context, Stranas KA 2045 strategic priorities, and cultural norms. While elements of the model (action learning + SFIA + Hexa-Helix) may generalize to other developing economies, direct replication without substantial localization is inadvisable.

### 8.3 Future Research Directions

**Rigorous Impact Evaluation**  
A priority is rigorous quasi-experimental evaluation comparing bootcamp participants with carefully matched control populations, tracking outcomes over 24–36 months. Outcome measures should include: employment stability, wages, workplace AI literacy, firm-level productivity, service delivery quality improvements, and social mobility indicators.

**Longitudinal Tracking of Cohort Progression**  
NUSA targets L2→L3 progression in the bootcamp; post-bootcamp outcomes may enable L3→L4 progression. Longitudinal tracking of 500+ participants over 3–5 years will illuminate the degree to which bootcamp experience catalyzes ongoing capability development.

**Comparative Analysis: Bootcamp vs. Traditional Upskilling**  
Systematic comparison of NUSA bootcamp outcomes with traditional government training, university programs, and employer training provides evidence on relative effectiveness and cost-efficiency of different upskilling modalities.

**Cultural and Contextual Adaptation Research**  
While pilot deployments occurred across Indonesian provinces with varying cultural contexts, systematic investigation of how pedagogical approaches, problem framing, and institutional engagement strategies might be adapted for different regional and cultural contexts (e.g., Javanese vs. Papuan cultural norms) is warranted.

**Agentic AI Skill Requirements**  
As agentic AI systems become more prevalent (autonomous agents performing complex tasks with minimal human direction), the specific competency requirements for human workers will evolve. Research tracking how SFIA competency requirements change as agentic AI deployment increases will inform curriculum evolution and keep NUSA frameworks current.

## 9. Conclusion

The NUSA Framework represents an integrated response to a critical policy challenge: how can developing economies like Indonesia conduct mass workforce upskilling in artificial intelligence rapidly enough to meet labor market transformation timelines, while maintaining pedagogical quality and institutional sustainability?

The framework's core contribution lies in its integration of four established scholarly traditions—action learning (Revans), competency-based education (SFIA), institutional innovation (Hexa-Helix), and contemporary AI workforce research—into a coherent operational model designed specifically for provincial-scale deployment within a developing economy context. Through the IRIS Cycle's four-phase pedagogical engine, NUSA operationalizes action learning in time-compressed bootcamp formats. Through SFIA alignment, it provides internationally legible competency definitions while remaining locally grounded. Through Hexa-Helix grounding, it embeds upskilling within existing institutional structures, increasing sustainability. Through ISO/IEC 17024 compliance pathways, it targets credibility and international recognition.

Initial pilot results from 2,500 participants across five provinces demonstrate feasibility of the model's core operational parameters: 94% attendance rates, 89% passing rate, 96% employment retention/transition, and 78% solution deployment to authentic institutional settings. Participant satisfaction and Net Promoter Score metrics (4.3/5.0, NPS 68) indicate strong program endorsement. Most critically, Provincial AI Task Forces have committed to second-cohort funding and ongoing implementation, suggesting institutional capacity and stakeholder buy-in.

However, significant research and implementation challenges remain. Rigorous counterfactual evaluation is needed to definitively attribute observed outcomes to the NUSA intervention. Longer-term tracking (24–36 months) is required to assess employment stability, wage progression, and firm-level productivity impacts. Scaling from 2,500 pilot participants to 100,000+ full deployment will encounter heterogeneous institutional contexts, capacity constraints, and governance challenges. Agentic AI's rapid evolution may necessitate ongoing competency framework refinement.

Despite these caveats, the NUSA Framework advances our understanding of how pedagogical innovation, standards alignment, and institutional design can jointly address the scale and urgency of workforce transformation in developing economies. Its combination of theoretical grounding, operational specificity, and early validation creates a foundation for both continued learning and for replication within Indonesia and, selectively, in other developing economy contexts.

The window for action is constrained. AI-driven labor market transformation is accelerating; developing economies that fail to build rapidly scalable, quality upskilling capacity risk profound labor market disruption and increased inequality. NUSA is designed not as a perfect solution, but as an evidence-based, iteratively improvable approach to meeting this urgent challenge. Success will require sustained commitment from Indonesian policymakers, development partners, educators, industry leaders, and—most fundamentally—from the workers and communities whose capability and agency ultimately determine whether national AI strategies translate into shared prosperity.
